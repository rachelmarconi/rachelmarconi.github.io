Pharmacy Deserts in Prince George's County, Maryland
Published Dec. 15, 2021, by Rachel Logan
1 Introduction
For this project, a rating system was crafted to analyze how accessible pharmacies are to the residents of each census tract in Prince George’s County in Maryland.
1.1 Motivation
On Oct. 1, 2021, a law came into effect [1] in the state removing the size and type ban for what over-the-county medication can be sold in vending machines – a ban that has been in place since at least the 1950’s. Despite strong proponents for the law among those in the Legislature, noting equity as a strong reason to allow vending machines for OTC pharmaceuticals, the author could find no agency, whether local or state, defining a plan to actually place any new vending machines. The hope is that this research will lay the foundation for pinpointing the most effective locations for pharmacy vending machines where the most load will be lifted.
1.2 Background
Food deserts have come up more in recent years, including research by both the United States Department of Agriculture and countywide agencies, but the research on pharmacy or healthcare deserts is much less built up. Two pharmacy vending machines exist at malls in the state, but those had not expanded to include more OTC medications or sell them in other than travel sizes by the time the author’s research was conducted.
1.3 Summary of Project
Using pharmacy location data from an open-source website and census tract geometry from R’s tidycensus package, average distances were found from census tract centroids to the closest three pharmacies. This distance served as a base point for determining walking and driving distances for the average resident in that census tract. No census tract in the county, surprisingly, surpassed my flagging threshold of a 15-minute drive on average to the closest three pharmacies, but many more were flagged for walking distance. As expected, many of the flagged tracts were in the outskirts of Washington, D.C., but several more were located in more spread-out areas of the county, far from the city. When combined with several other demographic points, including number of households without vehicles and the total population of a census tract, I identified 19 tracts of serious inaccessibility and 73 tracts with moderate inaccessibility.
1.4 Specific Contributions
The end product of this project is two-fold. A five-point accessibility rating system was developed and can be applied to counties across the nation to find areas still in need of accessibility efforts. Secondly, a map was produced reflecting the most in-need areas in the county, which can be forwarded to community groups and state delegates as a stepping stone for change.
2 Literature Review
A 2016 Capital News Service collaboration project [3] surveyed and reported on pharmacy deserts in Baltimore, reviewing testimony from residents and highlighting just how bad the problem is. This project takes it one step further by roping in pharmacy vending machines as a solution.

A September 2021 report by GoodRx [2] surveyed healthcare deserts nationwide last year as the COVID-19 pandemic highlighted health disparities. While that report analyzed every county in the country for hospital, emergency room and pharmacy deserts, among others, it erased changes within counties. It also considered only driving time and not walking time, and used 25 mph as a standard for even congested regions like Baltimore – and erased a prevalent issue. This project weighs walking time with household car ownership for a more accurate representation of pharmacy inaccessibility.
3 Methodology
This project combined both Excel manipulation and R coding to come up with distances.
3.1 Data Description
Pharmacy data had to be collected externally, while census geometry and demographic data were available with API access.
3.1.1 Collection and Curation
Two pharmacy location datasets were tested.

A County Office dataset collected all pharmacies in and around Prince George’s County. A web scraper was written in Selenium to scrape pharmacy names and locations across several pages of the online dataset. County Office states it is unaffiliated with any government agency but consolidates information across several agencies in each locality.
The County Office data, however, was out of date and selective as to which pharmacies were included. Addresses needed updates, or locations had been closed entirely. New pharmacies that have popped up in the last few years were missing.

Another dataset was held by Statesman Journal, which is under the USA Today umbrella. That source lists prescription pill order totals sent to each location in a county as part of a Washington Post project on Automation of Reports ad Consolidated Orders System (ARCOS) data researching the local and regional effects of the opioid crisis.

The pharmacies listed in this dataset are only those receiving hydrocodone and oxycontin and is from 2012 at its most recent, and although it was much more inclusive and accurate than the County Office data, it was still not recent enough for this project.

Finally, the project moved to using SafeGraph data downloaded with a free academic account. The main issue with this dataset is that it included all pharmacies in the country and had to be refined in R to only not only include only those in Maryland, but those in just Prince George’s County, both in towns and in census areas. This data, however, is accurate to 2021, so it was worth dealing with the massive dataset.

Census data was also downloaded into R using the tidycensus API and 2010 boundaries for census tracts, since that is the most recent year geometric data is available. Demographic data is from Annual Census Survey 2018 data, still mapped to 2010 geometry. The project requires total population, white population and percent population without vehicles data.
3.1.2 Cleaning
Pharmacy addresses had to be matched using the U.S. Census Bureau’s online geocoding tool to find latitude and longitude data for each pharmacy. There was about a 70% success rate matching addresses to coordinates.

Census tracts were run through the PostGIS spatial transform function st_centroid to find center points, which were then unlisted to latitude and longitude columns.

White population per census tract was removed from total population to find the percent of non-white residents in that tract.

To find distances from each pharmacy to each census tract centroid, the dataset was transferred to Excel. The Haversine formula was applied – a distance formula for between two geometric coordinates that accounts for the curvature of the earth. This produces a grid of distances. The dataset was read back into R to find the top 3 closest distances for every census tract, pharmacy independent, which were averaged together.

This distances dataset could then be converted into walking and driving speed. A detour factor of 1.417 was applied, a factor used by the GoodRx report [2] and confirmed as a reasonable factor by United States standards to include corners and turns into routes. Walking speed was estimated at 3 mph to account for elderly or disabled walkers in any seasonal condition. Driving speed was set at 25 mph to account for mostly urban travel times, although this speed is less likely the closer one gets to D.C. and also less likely as one drives down any state route within the county.
3.2 Solution Framework
A five-point rating system was devised.

The first point was driving distance. For households with vehicles available, 15 minutes was chosen as a viable cut-off for reasonable time spent traveling to one of the nearest three pharmacies.

The second point was walking distance. For households without access to a vehicle, 15 minutes was also chosen as the threshold for reasonable walking time to one of the closest three pharmacies.

The third point was the sheer number of households in a census tract lacking access to a vehicle. Sheer magnitude was chosen instead of percent population because a census tract with 10,000 residents may have only 5% of its population without vehicles, but that still equates to 500 households without vehicle access. The cut-off for a point in this area was 300 households.

The fourth point was total population, and a threshold of 4,000 people was chosen, the median for census tract population in the county. The more individuals using a single pharmacy, the more load there is on that pharmacy, and the more likely it is that there will be shortages or excess strain on pharmacists to complete orders.

The fifth point was percent nonwhite population, and a threshold of 90% was chosen. Prince George’s County is a majority minority area, but some areas are more concentrated than others – and those areas should be a priority for officials to ameliorate healthcare inequities.
4 Experiment Result
When the five-point rating system for pharmacy accessibility was applied over each census tract, some individual results were surprising, although the overall results showed that there are problem areas that can be alleviated.
4.1 Experiment Setup
Census tract GEOIDs were set beside the tract’s data lineup for each point of the rating system in Excel. Then conditional formatting was applied to find cells with values over threshold values and colored cells were counted manually. The resulting scores were mapped onto a Datawrapper map of 2010 census tracts.
4.2 Results
Driving time, surprisingly, didn’t catch a single point across the county. The longest drive time was in the Aquasco area in the south east of the county, a large area that has over 1,500 residents and 97% of households having vehicle access. The average drive time in Aquasco to the average three closest pharmacies was 14.7 minutes, just under the threshold. Many census tracts came up with drives of 3 to 7 minute, on average. Regardless, the point stayed included in the rating system in case it is applied in more rural areas.

Walk time, on the other hand, ranged from just 2 minutes in one tract to 16 or 17 minutes just outside D.C. In some cases, it stretched to half an hour or longer.
4.3 Findings
Nineteen census tracts out of 218 scored 4 out of 5 points, the worst possible in the county for pharmacy accessibility. A total of 73 scored 3 or 4, a third of all census tracts, indicating that many areas would benefit from increased pharmacy access.

Many of these areas were just outside of D.C, but many of those which scored 3 of 5 points were in the central county, including Largo and areas of Upper Marlboro.

Only 18 census tracts scored a perfect 0, in smatterings and tiny areas in Laurel, College Park and near Camp Springs.
5 Limitations
Several points held this rating system back. For one, it disregards the existence of bus and metro systems, which are used much more frequently in areas where households have less accessibility to vehicles. The issue is that bus and metro lines are difficult to map to coordinates, and it would be complex to condense distances between bus stops to calculate new pharmacy accessibility. Time between bus stops would still have to be considered.

It would help this project if 2020 geometries could be used for new census tracts, with new centroids. The shapes are different to account for a changing population.

The project does not consider a map that can display both rating data and location landmarks or outlines, making it difficult for non-county natives to understand what areas are pinpointed.
6 Conclusion and Future Work
Using this information on pharmacy deserts in the county, several paths can be taken to invite change. The first is to contact community centers in the hardest-hit census tracts and explain the good a pharmacy vending machine would do to bring OTC products to their neighbors. The next would be to contact legislative representatives to request grant money for initial costs of such a machine. The last would be to include local journalists and tell the stories of those in these neighborhoods and report on how they operate to get around pharmacy deserts.
